What Little Lens is

Not another enrichment class.
Something your child will actually remember.

There is a particular kind of stillness that happens when a child points a camera at something they genuinely care about. They stop performing. They start paying attention. That is what Little Lens is trying to create.

Documentary filmmaking teaches children to observe before they speak, to ask before they assume, and to stay with something long enough to understand it. These aren't skills you can drill. They come from doing something real.

Every child makes their own short documentary — a story they chose, filmed in the world, edited by their own hand. Not a group project. Not a polished product. A genuine first attempt at telling the truth about something.

Five children per cohort. Joy runs every session personally. The craft she teaches is the same one she practises.

Who's behind it

Joy Lee is a professional documentary filmmaker whose work has screened at IDFA Amsterdam, won Best Documentary at SIFF, and aired on Discovery Channel. She has spent over a decade telling real stories on film.

Little Lens is her way of sharing what that practice actually feels like — with children who are ready to try it.

What they carry with them

Not a certificate.
A way of seeing.

Documentary filmmaking produces a film. But what it really produces is a child who has learned to slow down, pay attention, and take something seriously from beginning to end. The film is the evidence. The habits are the point.

01
The habit of observation

Before they can film anything, they have to notice it. Documentary teaches children to look at the world with genuine curiosity — not to perform engagement, but to actually feel it.

02
The ability to really listen

Conducting an interview with a real person is different from answering questions in class. You have to hear what someone actually said — not what you expected them to say. That is a skill many adults never develop.

03
The discipline of follow-through

A documentary has a beginning, a middle, and an end. Your child drives the whole thing — from choosing the story to standing in front of an audience and presenting it. Nobody does that part for them.

04
A finished film that is theirs

Not a draft. Not a group project. A short documentary researched, filmed, and edited by your child — about a story they chose. Something real to keep, and something real to build on.

05
A different relationship with their phone

The concern with screentime isn't really the device — it's what children are doing with it. Scrolling and watching are passive; the phone harvests their attention. Documentary flips this completely. The phone becomes a tool pointed outward at the world, and your child is the one deciding where to look, what matters, what to keep. A child who has spent 90 minutes interviewing someone they find genuinely interesting is not the same child who reaches for a phone to fill silence. That shift is real, and it stays.

Who's behind Little Lens

Hi, I'm Joy.
I make documentaries.

I've spent over a decade making documentaries for Discovery Channel, government agencies, and DesignSingapore. My thesis film won Best Documentary at the Singapore International Film Festival and screened at IDFA Amsterdam. I've made films about female ex-offenders finding their way back, about people sitting with mortality, about Singapore craftspeople whose work deserved to be seen.

Little Lens grew from something I kept noticing: children have genuine, unguarded points of view about the world — and they rarely get the chance to develop those views into something real. When a child points a camera at something they actually care about, something shifts. They stop waiting to be told what to think and start figuring it out for themselves.

I'm not trying to build a franchise. I run every session personally, with five children at most. If it can't be small, it can't be what I'm describing.

I can promise that your child will be taken seriously, challenged gently, and taught by someone who still makes the thing she teaches. What they get from it will be their own.

This should be for everyone.

If cost is a barrier for your family, email Joy directly at hi.littlelens@gmail.com. We'll find a way.

For older students aged 14 and above: if you're serious about documentary filmmaking but financial means are limited, show us. Send something you've made — phone footage is fine, rough cuts welcome. We're looking for a genuine eye and real curiosity. Not production value.
