What Little Lens is

Documentary filmmaking
is a craft worth keeping.

There is something irreplaceable about teaching a child to observe the world carefully, to find a story in it, and to tell that story honestly. That is what documentary filmmakers do. It is also, quietly, one of the most rigorous things a young person can learn to do.

Little Lens isn't an enrichment class. There are no assigned topics, no templates, no shortcut to a polished result. Kids choose their own subject, conduct real interviews, and make every creative decision themselves — from the first story pitch to the final cut.

What they leave with is a finished film they made. And a set of skills — attention, curiosity, follow-through, the ability to listen — that transfers everywhere.

The AI component

We don't teach kids
to avoid AI.
We teach them to lead it.

AI is a working tool in documentary filmmaking — and treating it as anything else would shortchange what kids are here to learn.

What we teach is discernment: the ability to know when AI is useful, when it's wrong, and when it simply cannot do the thing you need done.

There are things AI will never do. It won't show up at a market at 7am. It won't notice the way someone's hands move when they talk about something they love. It won't know when a subject is about to say something true.

AI proposes. You decide. You rewrite. You own it.
  • Use AIGenerate research questions — then find the real answers yourself
  • Use AISummarise a long article — but trace every fact to the original source
  • Use AISuggest a script structure — then rewrite every line in your own voice
  • Use AITranscribe interviews — saving hours, not replacing thinking
  • Never AIObserve, feel, go outside, or talk to a real person
  • Never AINotice the unscripted moment that changes everything
  • Never AIKnow when someone is about to say something true

Who's behind Little Lens

Hi, I'm Joy.
I make documentaries.

I've spent over a decade telling real stories on film. My thesis documentary That's Wicked! won Best Documentary at the Singapore International Film Festival and screened at the International Documentary Film Festival Amsterdam. Since then I've directed and produced documentaries for Discovery Channel, government agencies, and DesignSingapore — including 24 documentaries on the Asian Insights programme.

I'm particularly proud of two 5-part series: We Are All Escapists (2019), which follows female ex-offenders in Singapore, and Fade to Black (2020), an exploration of mortality and the people it touches.

Little Lens was born from a simple belief — that every child has a story worth telling, and that learning to tell it honestly is one of the most powerful things we can teach them.

No two documentaries are the same

Documentary filmmaking isn't a formula. It's a form — and within that form, every filmmaker finds their own voice. That's what we teach kids to discover.

Observational

The camera watches without interfering. Just life as it happens.

Participatory

The filmmaker steps in — asking, provoking, becoming part of the story.

Poetic

Mood, rhythm, and image over argument. Some films just need to make you feel something.

Investigative

A puzzle to be solved. Following a thread wherever it leads.

Performative

The filmmaker's own experience becomes the lens.

Expository

A clear argument, evidence marshalled to persuade. Classic and direct.

At Little Lens, we don't tell kids which style to use. We show them all of them — and let them find the one that feels like theirs.
Questions worth asking

Before you join
the waitlist.

Do kids need any prior experience?
None at all. The curriculum assumes no prior knowledge of filmmaking, editing, or AI tools — just curiosity and a willingness to try.
What equipment does my child need?
A smartphone is enough for filming. For Documentary Makers and Advanced Documentarians, a laptop or tablet is needed for editing sessions — CapCut and iMovie are both free.
My child is shy. Is this still a good fit?
Documentary filmmaking shifts the focus outward — onto a subject, a story, someone else's world. Many quieter kids find it easier to speak when they're presenting their own film rather than answering a question in class.
How does the online format work?
Most sessions are online. One session is an on-location shoot day where kids film at a real location with Joy present. The final session includes a parent screening where kids present their finished documentary themselves.
Is this useful for DSA applications?
It can be — particularly for media arts, film, and visual arts talent tracks. SOTA explicitly lists film and photography as DSA talent areas, and a number of secondary schools accept DSA through Visual Arts and Design / Media tracks. We'd encourage you to check the specific requirements of the schools your child is applying to.
What does the AI component actually involve?
Kids use AI as a working tool in the filmmaking process — for research, transcription, and structural thinking. They learn to verify AI-generated information, rewrite AI-suggested copy in their own voice, and understand what AI cannot do. AI literacy in the context of real creative work, not a prompt-engineering class.
How small are the cohorts?
Maximum 5 kids per cohort. Documentary filmmaking requires individual attention — Joy works with each child on their own story, their own subject, their own film.
My child is between age groups. Which workshop?
Use maturity and independence as your guide more than age. A confident, self-directed 9-year-old may do well in Documentary Makers. Feel free to email Joy directly — she's happy to have a quick conversation.
